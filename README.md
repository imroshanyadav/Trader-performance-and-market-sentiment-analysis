Project Description
This project analyzes the relationship between Bitcoin market sentiment (Fear & Greed Index) and trader performance on Hyperliquid, using 211,224 trade records from 32 accounts (May 2023–May 2025) merged with the daily Fear & Greed classification (2018–2025).
Approach: Trade timestamps were mapped to calendar dates and joined to that day's sentiment label (99.997% match rate). Trades with non-zero Closed PnL were treated as "realized" outcomes and analyzed for win rate, average/total PnL, position size, and long/short bias — all grouped by sentiment regime (Extreme Fear, Fear, Neutral, Greed, Extreme Greed).
Key findings:

Win rates and average PnL per trade peak at emotional extremes — Extreme Greed (89.2% win rate) and Fear (87.3%) — while Extreme Fear and Greed (the more transitional regimes) underperform.
Traders show a contrarian directional bias: predominantly long during Fear (69%), flipping to predominantly short during Greed (55–58%) — buying capitulation and fading euphoria rather than chasing momentum.
The largest trades cluster disproportionately in Fear (46% of top 1% trades vs. a 29% baseline share) while position size shrinks sharply in Extreme Greed — suggesting disciplined risk scaling rather than FOMO-driven exposure.
Effects are asset-dependent: some tokens reward riding sentiment momentum, others (like ETH) reward fading it, so any sentiment-based rule should be asset-aware.

Deliverables: A formatted Word report (executive summary, methodology, 5 supporting charts, data tables, strategic recommendations, and limitations) plus the full merged dataset (trade-level data joined with daily sentiment) for further exploration.
Note: The brief referenced a "leverage" field that wasn't present in the actual data export — trade size (USD) was used as the closest available proxy for risk appetite, and this substitution is documented transparently in the report.
